"Isn't a fine the end of it as a criminal penalty?" Quite apart from the penalty imposed by the prosecution and the court, immigration reviews the same case again through a status review, and if the fine amount and the type of offence cross the fixed thresholds, it issues a removal disposition (a departure order or compulsory deportation). Below, we set out those thresholds and how to respond.

The criminal penalty and the status review

The criminal process and the immigration process run separately.

When a fine becomes final in court, the result is passed to immigration, which re-decides, on the same case, whether the person should be allowed to keep residing in Korea. This is the immigration status review (사범심사).

A removal measure can be decided separately from the criminal penalty, and that decision is made on the basis of the fine amount and the type of offence.

Departure order vs. compulsory deportation

"Removal" is the umbrella term for measures that send a person out of Korea, enforced in two ways.

A departure order (출국명령) is the lighter disposition that gives you the chance to leave on your own. You are not held in a detention center; you depart yourself within a set deadline.

A compulsory deportation (강제퇴거) is forced removal, with detention and a long-term re-entry ban. A sentence of imprisonment or heavier makes you subject to compulsory deportation. Which way it is enforced determines the re-entry restriction and other later consequences.

Removal thresholds by fine amount

Immigration treats a foreigner who has received a fine as subject to removal where any of the following applies.

  • A first offence sentenced to a fine of ₩3 million or more
  • Fines totalling ₩5 million or more within the last 5 years
  • Two or more fines within the last 2 years, or three or more within 5 years
  • A traffic-related fine of ₩5 million or more
  • A fine for drug or sexual offences, etc. (a departure order regardless of number or amount)

Crossing a threshold does not automatically fix the outcome. The reviewing officer also considers whether it is a first offence, whether you have family in Korea, how long you have lived here, and whether a settlement was reached. Where those circumstances are organized and substantiated with evidence, a compulsory deportation may be mitigated to a departure order.

Serious crimes and the permanent entry ban

Serious crimes are not about the amount. For offences immigration classifies as serious, even a disposition as light as a suspension of indictment can result in removal together with a permanent entry ban.

  • Insurrection and treason
  • Murder; rape and indecent acts; robbery
  • Violations of the Act on Special Cases Concerning the Punishment of Sexual Crimes
  • Violations of the Narcotics Control Act
  • Violations of the Act on the Aggravated Punishment of Specific Crimes (abduction/inducement, habitual robbery/theft, repeat robbery causing injury, retaliatory crimes, drug offenders)
  • Violations of the Punishment of Violences Act
  • Violations of the Act on the Control of Public Health Crimes

In addition, immigration also classifies the following as serious crimes.

  • Special obstruction of official duties
  • Sexual-violence and child/youth sexual-abuse offences
  • Dangerous driving causing injury
  • Voice phishing, messenger phishing, romance scams and similar fraud committed via information and communications networks
  • Crimes causing death (excluding negligence and occupational negligence)

Even for the same fine, if the offence falls within this list it results in removal and a permanent entry ban regardless of amount.

The status review after the criminal penalty

Once the fine is final, the case is reported to immigration, and the immigration office calls the person in for a status review. The result of that review decides whether a departure order or compulsory deportation issues.

A statement made at this examination stage becomes the basis for the disposition, and once a statement is set, it is difficult to correct later.

With counsel's assistance, your lawyer can attend the immigration examination with you — preventing mistranslation, organizing which circumstances to state and in what order, and preparing supporting evidence. What is reflected in the examination is not a claim that "I never broke the law," but evidence showing that the disposition would be excessive.

Once the examination produces a disposition, the deadline is short. An objection to a departure order or compulsory deportation must be filed within 7 days of receiving the disposition. If the objection is dismissed, you file a revocation lawsuit in the administrative court within 90 days. A lawsuit alone does not halt departure, so to stop it you must also apply for a stay of execution.

A departure order converts to unlawful stay if you do not leave within the deadline (usually 30 days). Compulsory deportation becomes hard to contest during detention, so responding from the examination stage is important.

Detention and temporary release

In the compulsory-deportation process you are held in an immigration detention center until departure. With movement and contact restricted, preparing on your own becomes difficult at the very point you need to contest the disposition.

Here you can apply for temporary release from detention, which can free you for a limited time before the process ends. Whether it is granted turns on how well you can show there is no flight risk — through your residence, family, and the need to prepare litigation.

Re-entry restrictions after removal

If you leave under a departure order, the re-entry ban is 1–5 years depending on the case; for compulsory deportation it is at least 5 years.

If the case is classified as a serious crime above, it is a permanent entry ban, and re-entry is restricted in principle.

That said, where you have a Korean spouse or a minor child, or need significant medical treatment, re-entry permission may exceptionally be granted even during the ban period. If you are preparing to return, first confirm the content of your disposition and the length of your restriction.

Frequently asked questions

If a foreigner gets a ₩3 million fine, are they deported?
A first offence with a fine of ₩3 million or more is subject to removal. The cumulative (₩5M over 5 years), frequency, and traffic (₩5M) criteria also apply.
What happens with a fine for a drug or sexual offence?
It is subject to a departure order regardless of amount or count. If classified as a serious crime, it leads to removal together with a permanent entry ban.
Can a fine result in compulsory deportation?
A fine can make you subject to removal, enforced as a departure order or compulsory deportation depending on the case. A sentence of imprisonment or heavier results in compulsory deportation.
I received a departure order — can I contest it?
Yes. An objection within 7 days and administrative litigation within 90 days; to halt departure you also apply for a stay of execution. The deadlines are short, so respond immediately.
How long after removal can I not re-enter?
A departure order carries 1–5 years; compulsory deportation at least 5 years; serious crimes a permanent ban. With family or treatment grounds, re-entry permission may exceptionally be granted.
